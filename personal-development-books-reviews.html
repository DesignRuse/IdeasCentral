Can't Hurt Me: Master Your Mind and Defy the Odds

by David Goggins

David Goggins' childhood was a living nightmare, with poverty, bigotry, and physical abuse colouring his days and haunting his nights.

David Goggins is one of the world's toughest people. He was abused as a youngster, both physically and emotionally, but instead of accepting his circumstances, he exploited them to propel him to greatness.

Goggins wanted to join the Air Force, but when he looked in the mirror, all he saw was a child with limited physical and mental capabilities. He was suffering from toxic stress, which resulted in learning disabilities and significant social anxiety. Instead of accepting his situation, he opted to hold himself accountable using the mirror. He had lost 106 pounds in three months and was ready to face anything.

Goggins wanted to show his trainers and coaches that he was prepared to go above and beyond their expectations. So he invented a technique known as the cookie jar to help him overcome these obstacles. The cookie jar reminds you of all the difficulties you've overcome in the past.

According to Goggins, most persons barely use 40% of their potential. To become the superhero that he is, Goggins had to put in a lot of work and commitment. In 'Can't Hurt Me', he's designed a three-week challenge to help you reach your full potential. Lessons include coping with your brain's governor, embracing failure, and leveraging prior successes as a springboard for new endeavours.

Our Score: 9/10

The Courage To Be Disliked: How to free yourself, change your life and achieve real happiness

by Ichiro Kishimi and Fumitake Koga

To be content, one must be prepared to be disliked. When you have that courage, all of your interpersonal encounters will resemble one another. This book is based on the ideas of Alfred Adler, an Austrian doctor and psychologist. It teaches you how to make great life adjustments and achieve real happiness.

There are three major takeaways from the book...

  • Your past does not determine your future, and all that is required is a willingness to change. The first step is to believe in the possibility of change.
  • Many of us focus on our flaws or lacks and blame them for where we are in life. Adler categorises all of these issues and flaws into two categories: objective and subjective. Objective things are those that can be measured and are objectively true. Subjective faults are those that people manufacture; they are not actual.
  • According to Adler, community should include every single person in the world. If you focus on yourself and treat others like partners and equals, you can achieve anything. You will be more happier in your life if you let go of that competitive, limited attitude and embrace the concept of plenty and the potential of achievement for everyone.
